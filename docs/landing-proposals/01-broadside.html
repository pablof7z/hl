A Notice
to Thinking Readers

§

Concerning a small room, built for the slow way of reading.

April, 2026

The internet had an idea and lost it somewhere between the group chat and the algorithm. We are writing to propose getting it back.

The idea, plainly, was that a handful of people whose taste you trust could read a book or an article together and talk it through. Not broadcast. Not performance. Not a thread you lose in the scroll. The older thing — salon, reading circle, kitchen table, whatever you want to call it. The kind of conversation that is slower than it is clever, and better for being slower.

That conversation still exists, plainly. It just does not happen online. It happens in someone's living room, in a book club that meets on the last Thursday of the month, in a two-year text thread between six friends, in marginalia passed back and forth between a teacher and a student. The internet, which solved the problem of finding anyone to talk to, never bothered to solve the problem of finding the particular five people who would make the conversation good.

It is 2026 and this is getting worse. The commons is filling with machine writing. The threshold for what is worth reading is rising. The tools most people have for discussing what they read are a WhatsApp group, a Notion doc, and a good memory. None of them is designed for the thing being asked of it. All of them fail in the same way, which is that the conversation washes downstream and takes the specific passage it was about with it.

Anything worth discussing deserves a room smaller than the internet.

What this is

A room, which is to say an invite-only space that six to twenty people share. Inside the room, one piece of writing at a time sits at the center — a book everyone is reading, an essay nobody can put down, a two-hour conversation the room should all listen to this week. The members mark the passages they want to keep. Everyone sees what everyone else marked. The discussion happens next to the passages, not in a separate thread that scrolls away from what it is about.

There is no feed. There is no audience. Your highlights are not scored by an algorithm. No strangers grade your takes. Nobody is going to go viral on Highlighter. That is, as it happens, the point.

The mechanics

  1. You start a room. Give it a name. Invite five people, or twelve, or fifteen. Closed by default. Private if you want, or public if the room is the kind of room whose existence is part of the point.
  2. Someone shares what the room is reading this week. A book. An essay. A podcast episode. A white paper. Whoever is paying attention, pays attention, and the conversation starts to cluster around the passage.
  3. You highlight as you go. What caught you, what you disagree with, what you need to return to. Your friends see the excerpts you pulled. You see theirs. The margin is where the room lives.
  4. That is the product. There is no phase two. No gamified streak, no weekly summary, no algorithmic re-ranking of which highlight "performed best." Just the book and the people and what they pulled from it.

Who this is for

You know five to fifteen people whose reading you would rather watch than most published criticism. You already send them links. You already wish the conversation did not die in the DM. You have tried to run this on WhatsApp and Discord and Notion and Slack and a shared Google Doc, and it always dies the same way: the thread outlives its subject by a week, and then both are gone.

If that is you, you are the reader this is for. If it isn't, we are not insulted — the room is probably not for everyone, and pretending otherwise is how the last version of this idea got itself into trouble.

Who this is not for

  • Anyone optimizing for discovery, reach, or audience. There is none of that here by design.
  • Anyone who wants a reading app that nudges, streaks, or congratulates them for finishing. This is a room, not a coach.
  • Anyone whose preferred conversation about books is a single cold take posted to a feed of strangers.

Terms of membership

Your identity is a key you own. Your rooms are not trapped here; they live on an open protocol and can move to another host if we go dark. (We have been in this neighborhood before. We know what a shutdown email feels like. We built this so nobody reading in one of these rooms would ever need to receive one.) Your data is your data. Your members are your members. We do not mine, rank, or sell any of it.

Rooms open in small waves. If someone sent you this, you already have a way in. If nobody did, you can request a card below, and we will write back when there is space.
