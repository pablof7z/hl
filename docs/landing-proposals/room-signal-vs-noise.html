On re-reading Postman in 2026

This is my third time through Amusing Ourselves to Death. The first was 2009, the second 2016. Both times I read it as history. This time, reading it in 2026 with LLMs reshaping the attention economy in real time, it reads as forecast.

The move I missed both times: Postman is not arguing that the medium determines the message. He's arguing that the dominant medium determines what a culture can know. Print-based culture knows some things. Television-based culture cannot know them. What we're now watching, I think, is the formation of a culture that can only know what an algorithm can optimise for.

Curious if the room agrees.

Chapter 5 and the shape of what's next

Chapter 5 is the chapter I've been thinking about all week. Postman is making the case that the telegraph broke a particular shape of public discourse. But the thing he's describing — the decontextualisation of information, the separation of facts from their use-cases — is the exact failure mode we're building Nostr to route around.

A highlight on Nostr is a recontextualisation. The excerpt + the signature + the group it's shared to — that's the “place” the information has. Postman would call this re-entry. I'd call it a mild cure.

Is this a book you disagree with or not?

I'm five chapters in and I still can't tell if this is a book I agree with. The argument is persuasive but Postman's implied prescription — television bad, print good — collapses into nostalgia whenever he gets specific. The real argument, I think, is not about television. It's about what any dominant medium does to thought.

Would love to know what the rest of the room thinks. DK, Pablo — you've been through this before.
