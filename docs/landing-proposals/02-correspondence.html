Somewhere, a Tuesday
in April, 2026

Dear you,

I've been meaning to tell you about this for a while, and I've been putting it off because I didn't want to oversell it and I didn't want to make you feel obligated. I'm going to try to split the difference.

You know the seven of us — Mara the translator, Ben who reads three things a week and won't shut up about any of them, Anjali who takes three months on a single book and is always right about it, Simon, Jo, Dora, and me. You've heard me talk about this group enough to know we are insufferable about what we read and devoted to each other in the way that only people who've argued about the same books for eight years can be.

For most of the last two years our group has lived in a beautiful mess. A WhatsApp thread that scrolled faster than anyone could keep up with. A Google Doc that grew to forty-two pages and stopped being readable in month three. A Substack we tried and quietly abandoned. Two Zoom calls that happened out of the six we scheduled. We all loved each other and we kept losing the thread, literally. I cannot tell you how many times someone has asked, wait, where did Ben make that point about Middlemarch, and the answer was, it is forty-two Thursdays ago in a WhatsApp group, I'm not going to find it for you.

In January we moved to something called Highlighter, and I want to tell you what it's like.

It is a room. That's the part that matters first. Only the seven of us can see it. We put the book we're all reading at the top — this winter it was Middlemarch, and before that The Rings of Saturn, and before that a stretch of essays by Ursula Le Guin we'd all been meaning to read — and that's it, that's the room. No feed. No stranger's takes. Nothing is being sold to us. Nobody is optimizing anything.

What you do inside the room is you mark the passages. You pull the lines that caught you. When Mara pulls a line, you see it; when you pull one, she sees it. The conversation — what did this mean, who has the better reading of Dorothea's first marriage, was this sentence as devastating for you as it was for me — happens next to the passages. Not in a thread that scrolls away from the book. On the page.

That is the whole product. I'm not hiding a feature.

Here is what I can tell you honestly: our Middlemarch discussion this winter was the best sustained conversation the seven of us have had since graduate school. I know some of that is the book. I know some of it is that we've gotten older and are finally interesting. But some of it, I'm telling you, is that we could find the specific passage Ben was making his point about, and Mara's reply was under Ben's reply, which was under the sentence in the book, which meant the conversation kept the book and never became chatter about a book.

Nobody is trying to get us to read faster. Nobody is making us finish. Anjali is seventy pages in and has made the most interesting highlights of anyone. That's fine. Nothing breaks.

I don't know if it's for you, and I'm deliberately not selling it. What I can say is that if you ever wanted a version of our group — four of your own people, whose taste you'd follow into a difficult book — this is the thing that might finally make that actually happen. I've watched three friends try to start a book club this year and fail in the same way we did for two years. This is the thing we tried that worked.

If you want a card, I can get you one. You'd want to start a room with three or four of your own people. Not a broadcast list. Not an “everyone who's ever read a book with me.” Three or four people who will actually finish things with you.

If it doesn't sound like you, forget I wrote. I'll still text you the Le Guin.

Yours, as ever — L.
The stack is something called Nostr, which I assume means nothing to you and means little to me. What it does mean is that the rooms are not trapped on their servers. We've all received one too many “service is shutting down” emails. This one won't generate another.

To whoever's reading,

L. sent me this page to read and asked if I wanted to add anything. Here is what I want to add.

I joined because I wanted an excuse to read Proust with three specific people. I stayed because after we finished Proust we kept going. We are on Calvino now. I have not missed a week in eleven months. I could not tell you the last time I said that about anything on the internet.

Yours — J.
