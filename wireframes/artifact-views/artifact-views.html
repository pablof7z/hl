The Age of Curation: Why What You Choose to Highlight Defines You
by Maria Chen · Published in The Atlantic · June 2024
47 highlights 23 people 8 comments

There's a quiet revolution happening in how we consume information. Not in the writing — there's always been plenty of that — but in the act of choosing what matters. Every time you drag your cursor across a sentence, you're making a statement about what you value.12

For centuries, marginalia was a private act. Readers scribbled in the margins of books they owned, creating a shadow text that lived alongside the author's words. These notes were intimate, conversational, sometimes combative. Fermat's last theorem began as a margin note — a private aside that became one of mathematics' greatest challenges.8

The Social Margin

What happens when those margins become public? When your highlights aren't just for you, but for everyone?

The answer is that curation becomes a form of expression. Your highlights are a fingerprint — a unique pattern that says as much about you as the words you choose to write yourself. We are entering an age where the curator is as important as the creator.23

Consider how you already do this informally. When you share an article, you're curating. When you quote-tweet a passage, you're highlighting. When you tell a friend "you have to read this part," you're directing attention — the scarcest resource of our time.

The difference now is that these acts of curation can be persistent, layered, and collective. A single passage might be highlighted by a physicist, a poet, and a programmer — each for entirely different reasons.15 And in seeing those overlapping highlights, we glimpse something rare: the same words refracted through different minds.

Collective Intelligence

There's a deeper implication here. When thousands of people read the same text and independently highlight the same passage, that convergence creates a signal. It's a form of collective intelligence — not about what's true, but about what resonates.31

This isn't the wisdom of crowds in the prediction-market sense. It's something more subtle. It's the emergence of shared meaning from individual acts of attention. Every highlight is a tiny vote for "this matters," and the aggregate of those votes draws a map of what a community finds important, beautiful, or provocative.19

Traditional metrics tell us what was consumed. Highlights tell us what was felt. The difference is everything.

We are moving from an economy of content to an economy of attention — and the people who help us direct that attention well are becoming the most valuable contributors in any intellectual community. Not the loudest voices, but the most discerning eyes.
